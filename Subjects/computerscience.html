Computer Science
Intro to Computer Science • AP Computer Science A • Project Course
Computer science is a booming industry over the past decades and it continues to grow in importance and relevance in this Information Age. One of the growing pains I have seen as a newcomer to this industry is hastily put together foundation to build up the youth for this field. My high school courses as well as the courses at schools I student taught at all had mediocre computer science departments where the teacher was often forced into that role. Quality teaching is fundamental to the development of the next generation, and I hope to be part of your joint effort to make sure this subject continues to flourish.

Programming is difficult. Let me revise that. Programming well is difficult, while programming in general is quite simple. There are two main hurdles I have seen all computer science students face. The first is learning how to break down the problem into logical steps. Thinking in logical steps that break down a problem into inputs and outputs is a process that is foreign to many. Similar to how people say they do not have a brain for math, this is the same type of hurdle. We have to build up and practice looking at problems from a different viewpoint to solve these problems. Once this is built up, programming is deeply intuitive and becomes a natural process in how we observe the natural world. Passing this hurdle is what makes a programmer who can solve problems and write code for a job. Programming well is the next hurdle where we learn how to optimize problems in an unconventional way to get the best solution. We have to dive deeper and deeper to find more elegant solutions and discover how vast this field truly is. I hope to guide students to find joy in discovery that fuels them to continue beyond my guidance. This drive to be better is what makes a good programmer.
Materials
There are a lot of resources I have used in my class. Here are some that I was comfortable introducing to a class.
  • Jupyter Notebook for beginner programming assignments that require more structure and smaller portions of code.
  • Processing or p5.js for graphical based projects tailored for introductory courses.
  • Visual Studo Code is my IDE of choice which can run nearly any language and is the standard for higher education and industry.
  • Github is a free cloud-based respository that allows for easy collaboration and submission of programming projects.
I develop my own teaching materials to ensure there is a consistant flow throughout the entire semester and the materials suit the current needs of the students. These are informed by my experience in my undergraduate studies, experience in the classroom, textbooks, and current industry advancements.
  • Slide show presentations
  • Published class notes for students to access
  • Video lessons for personalized instruction (Project Course)
Personal Style
To me, understanding is essential. Computer science is a directly applicable field of study, so I like to focus my efforts on self-sufficiency. Through a balance of cohesive lectures that introduce the new concept and practice with its application, students mimic how to gather resources and advocate for their own education.

During lectures, I engage students with open ended questions that provoke discussion abnd prior knowledge. These are often made from what I observed that they found to be difficult. They are also made to mimic how they should grow and what they should ask themselves to make sure they have a full grasp of the concepts.

I see education as a place where I join the students in exploration of the subject to show that I am on their side and a resource they should use to grow.
Intro to Computer Science
Objective

As an introductory course to the world of computer science, this course places an emphasis on developing a problem solving mindset within students.

This course will most likely be taught using Python and Jupyter Notebook.

Course Outline
(hover over the units)
  • Unit 1: Variables and Data Storing values is fundamental to programming. By using variables, values can be saved and modified to be referred by different people and programs. Naming guidelines and understanding limitations of each data type allows for efficient programming. In this unit, students will be introduced to pseudocode with the use of simple operations and variables.
  • Unit 2: Algorithmic Solutions All solutions are not equal, and finding the best solution is what makes a developer stand out. Procedural programming is a basic way of apporaching problems as they are solved step-by-step in order. Although it may not be the most efficient, it does begin developing a problem solving mindset that breaks down a complex scenario into step-sized components.
  • Unit 3: Functions Programming often involves a repetition of small tasks. For example, adding two values takes a lot of work on the computer's end, but we can simply use the addition symbol to tell the computer what to do. This unit dives into learning how to make instruction sets that can be generalized to solve a common problem. It expands the problem solving mindset to include thinking for other scenarios.
  • Unit 4: Strings Strings are what programmers call a sequence of characters. It is an unusual data type with how it is stored and how it can be used. In the beginning context, it is a familiar format that allows students to understand objects and the personal functions that an object can have. Projects will be centered around manipulating different strings and prepare students for the concept of arrays.
  • Unit 5: Conditionals Not all inputs are the same. Conditional statements allow us to now create a branching flow-chart to solve a problem. Variables of the problem are determined and comparisons are made to create a solution to a problem that produces the correct outputs. This is the last step that is fully procedural programming for the students.
  • Unit 6: Iterations To optimize a program and to allow it to go beyond human capabilities, there is the tool of iterations. Snippets of code are repeated over an over to manipulate data thousands of times to return a desired result. Problems can not be solved one step at a time now, but the problem has to be generalized to allow a pattern to be seen.
  • Unit 7: Arrays This final unit serves as an introduction to the wide array of data types that are used in higher levels of programming. Arrays are a collection of a singular data type which allows for larger programs. Projects can now include large amounts of data and the need for efficient programming can now be felt by the programmers.
